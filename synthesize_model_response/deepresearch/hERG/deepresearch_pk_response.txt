# Rules for Predicting hERG Channel Blockade

## Executive Summary

Human Ether-à-go-go-Related Gene (hERG) channel blockage by drug candidates is a major cardiotoxicity risk. Blockers are typically **lipophilic, basic, and planar** molecules that can bind deeply in the channel pore. Key strategies to reduce hERG risk include lowering lipophilicity and basicity, adding polar functional groups, and breaking planarity or aromaticity. For example, adding oxygen or sp² nitrogen to increase polarity, removing alkyl groups to decrease lipophilicity, or introducing a hydroxyl adjacent to a basic amine all strongly **decrease hERG inhibition**. Conversely, multiple aromatic rings, halogen substituents (especially F or Cl), and protonated tertiary amines at physiological pH tend to **increase hERG binding**. General thresholds reported include keeping calculated logP ≤4 (logD₇.₄ ≤3) and molecular weight ≤500 to aim for weak hERG affinity (IC₅₀ >10 μM). A combined metric `(clogP)² + (pKa)² < 110` has been recommended to flag risky compounds. The concise rules below summarize physicochemical and structural checks an experienced medicinal chemist should apply when evaluating or optimizing candidates for minimal hERG blockade risk.

## Key Physicochemical and Structural Rules

1. **Calculate clogP < 4 (LogD₇.₄ < 3.3 for neutral compounds)** – lower lipophilicity targets weaker hERG binding.
2. **Check (clogP)² + (pKa)² < 110** – combined lipophilicity and basicity metric to mitigate hERG liability.
3. **Check pKa of basic centers; keep pKa ≲ 7–8** to minimize positive charge at physiological pH.
4. **Calculate topological polar surface area (TPSA) > 75 Å²** to increase polarity and reduce hERG affinity.
5. **Calculate H-bond donor count ≥ 2** and **H-bond acceptor count ≥ 5** to increase polarity (reduces lipophilicity).
6. **Check molecular weight ≤ 500 Da** – lower molecular size tends to have lower hERG risk.
7. **Calculate aromatic ring count ≤ 3** – excess rings increases planarity and hERG binding.
8. **Check absence of fused bicyclic aromatics** (e.g. quinolines) to avoid rigid, planar scaffolds.
9. **Check if multiple halogens (especially F, Cl) are present** – halogenation often correlates with higher hERG potency.
10. **Calculate rotatable bond count > 5** – high flexibility tends to lower hERG binding (more flexible molecules fit less well).
11. **Check number of basic amines ≤ 2** – multiple basic centers (especially cationic at pH 7.4) increase hERG risk.
12. **Calculate distance between two basic centers**; avoid short distance that can create multivalent cationic binding motifs.
13. **Check for positively charged centers at pH 7.4** (e.g. protonated tertiary amines) – cationic groups strongly interact with the hERG pore.
14. **Check for presence of tertiary amines** – tertiary aliphatic amines frequently increase hERG affinity.
15. **Check presence of basic heterocycles** (e.g. imidazole, pyridine) – these may contribute to basicity and aromatic interactions.
16. **Calculate molecular dipole**; higher dipole (more polarized) usually correlates with less passive channel penetration (lower hERG risk).
17. **Check lipophilic efficiency (pIC₅₀ – clogP)**; a lower lipophilic efficiency often reflects safer profiles.
18. **Calculate clogD₇.₄ (or clogP)** – aim for clogD₇.₄ < 3 for minimize hERG potency.
19. **Check formal charge**; avoid permanently charged species (like quaternary ammoniums).
20. **Calculate cation–π interaction potential** – e.g. aromatic rings adjacent to cationic center elevate hERG binding.
21. **Check planarity/rigidity**; highly planar, rigid molecules (flat polyaromatics) tend to bind hERG strongly.
22. **Check basic center separation**; long distance between basic amine and aromatic ring may lower cation–π binding.
23. **Check presence of methanesulfonamide** (–SO₂CH₃) – this substructure often contributes to hERG blockade (remove/replace if present).
24. **Check presence of thiazole** – aromatic thiazole rings are common in hERG binders.
25. **Check presence of quinoline or isoquinoline** – fused benzheterocycles (quinoline) often increase hERG risk.
26. **Check for “structural alerts” known from literature** – e.g. aryl-guanidines, piperazine-phenyl, or other motifs known to block hERG (per SAR analyses).
27. **Calculate lipophilicity-per-potency balance** (e.g. **Lipophilic Ligand Efficiency** – LLE) – maintain good potency with the minimum lipophilicity to reduce hERG liability.

Each numerical rule above corresponds to one or more references in published hERG SAR and QSAR studies. For example, Jamieson *et al.* found that strong base and high logP are prime drivers of hERG inhibition. Matched-pair analyses (Springer *et al.*) showed that **increasing polarity (e.g. adding O or sp² N) and removing alkyl groups consistently reduced hERG IC₅₀ by 3–10-fold**. Importantly, placing a hydroxyl adjacent to a basic nitrogen (enabling intramolecular H-bonding) was never observed to *increase* hERG binding, and always decreased it. In contrast, features like multiple aromatic rings, halogens, and protonated tertiary amines were found in most blockers.

These diagrams illustrate how **increasing lipophilicity, basicity, aromaticity, halogenation, or positive charge** tends to raise hERG risk, whereas **adding polarity (HBA/HBD), flexibility (rotatable bonds), and reducing these factors** lowers risk. They also suggest medicinal chemistry strategies: if clogP or pKa is high, consider adding polar substituents or electron-withdrawing heterocycles, etc., which have been shown to attenuate hERG blockade.
