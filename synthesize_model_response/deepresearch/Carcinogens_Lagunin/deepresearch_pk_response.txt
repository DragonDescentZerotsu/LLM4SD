# Executive Summary
We compiled concise structural and physicochemical “rule-of-thumb” checks that flag molecular features known to correlate with carcinogenic potential. These rules draw on expert toxicology sources (IARC, EPA, OECD), carcinogenicity reviews (e.g. Benigni & Bossa), and structural-alert databases (Derek Nexus, Toxtree). The focus is on known DNA-reactive and promoting motifs (electrophiles, planar aromatics, etc.) and on key ADMET-related properties. For example, planar polyaromatic systems (PAHs) are known DNA intercalators activated to epoxides, while groups such as epoxides, aldehydes, aromatic amines, nitro/azo/nitroso, quinones, and S-/N-mustards are classic DNA-reactive alerts. Physicochemical limits (logP, MW, PSA, H-bond donors/acceptors) are included in analogy to known drug-like thresholds, since extreme values (e.g. very high lipophilicity or MW) tend to increase persistence and cellular penetration. Each rule is stated in “Calculate/Check” form (≤20 words) without explanation, per guidance. Table 1 summarizes all rules with their rationale sources and confidence (High/Medium/Low). In practice, one would apply these rules sequentially (see flowchart below) to flag structural alerts and property “red flags.” Figures 1 and 2 illustrate the decision workflow (mermaid flowchart) and the relative confidence of each rule. The report is addressed to medicinal chemists and emphasizes chemical reasoning over pure statistical models.

1. **Check** for *polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons* (≥3 fused aromatic rings).
2. **Check** for *planar aromatic systems* (flat π-conjugated rings, intercalation potential).
3. **Check** for *epoxide or aziridine* rings (strained 3-membered heterocycles).
4. **Check** for *alkyl halide or sulfate* groups (R–X or R–O–SO₂R; hard alkylators).
5. **Check** for *α,β-unsaturated carbonyl* (enone; Michael acceptor).
6. **Check** for *primary/secondary aromatic amine* (ArNH₂ or ArNHR).
7. **Check** for *nitroso or nitro* group (R–NO or R–NO₂, especially on aromatics).
8. **Check** for *azo* linkage (–N=N–, aromatic diazo).
9. **Check** for *quinone* moiety (ortho- or para-diketone on aromatic ring).
10. **Check** for *aldehyde* group (R–CHO).
11. **Check** for *S- or N-mustard* motif (geminal bis(haloalkyl)–S or –N).
12. **Check** for *polyhalogenated aromatics* (e.g. chlorinated/brominated benzene or dioxin-like rings).
13. **Calculate** logP; no strict cutoff (very high logP increases lipophilicity/persistence).
14. **Calculate** molecular weight (MW); no strict cutoff (extremely high MW implies poor clearance).
15. **Calculate** topological polar surface area (TPSA); no cutoff (low TPSA favors cell penetration).
16. **Calculate** H-bond donors/acceptors; no cutoff (few donors/acceptors favors permeability).
17. **Calculate** rotatable bonds; no cutoff (high flexibility may reduce binding/intercalation).
18. **Calculate** aromatic ring count; no cutoff (more rings → higher planar surface).
19. **Calculate** strongest acid/base pK_a; no cutoff (extreme pK_a yields ionic species, reducing membrane crossing).
20. **Check** for *redox-cycling potential* (e.g. catechol/quinone systems).
21. **Check** for *metabolic stability* flags (e.g. multiple rings, halogens; persistent scaffolds).
22. **Check** for *CYP-mediated bioactivation* motifs (e.g. aromatic N-heterocycles needing oxidation).
23. **Check** for *DNA-binding propensity* (flat polyaromatics, intercalators).
24. **Check** for general *genotoxic alerts* (presence of any of the above functional groups).

The table below summarizes each rule, its literature rationale, and an expert-assigned confidence level.

| Rule (calculate/check) | Rationale & Sources | Confidence |
|:----------------------------------------------------|:-----------------------------------------------------------------------|:-----------|
| 1. PAH (≥3 fused rings) | Polycyclic aromatics are DNA intercalators and form reactive epoxides | High |
| 2. Planar aromatic (flat π-system) | Planar aromatics intercalate DNA | High |
| 3. Epoxide/aziridine | Strained epoxides/aziridines are hard electrophiles that bind DNA | High |
| 4. Alkyl halide/sulfate | Alkyl halides/sulfates are strong alkylators (electrophilic) | High |
| 5. α,β-Unsaturated carbonyl | α,β-Enones (Michael acceptors) react with nucleophiles | High |
| 6. Aromatic amine (ArNH₂/ArNHR) | ArNH₂ → N-hydroxylation → DNA-binding nitrenium ion | High |
| 7. Aromatic nitro/nitroso | Nitro/nitroso arenes are bioreducible genotoxins | High |
| 8. Azo (–N=N–) | Azo linkages (aromatic diazo) are reduced to reactive amines | High |
| 9. Quinone (ortho/para-diketone) | Quinones undergo redox cycling and form adducts | High |
| 10. Aldehyde (–CHO) | Aldehydes are reactive carbonyl electrophiles | High |
| 11. S-/N-Mustard (gem-dihaloalkyl–S/N) | Mustards form aziridinium ions (DNA cross-linkers) | High |
| 12. Polyhalogenated aromatics | PCBs/dioxins (polyhalogenated aromatics) are carcinogens | Medium |
| 13. logP (partition coeff.) | Extreme logP (>5) indicates high lipophilicity, bioaccumulation | Medium |
| 14. Molecular weight | Very high MW correlates with persistence/low solubility | Medium |
| 15. TPSA (polar surface area) | Low TPSA favors cell uptake; high TPSA hinders permeability | Medium |
| 16. H-bond donors/acceptors | Fewer H-bond donors/acceptors increases membrane permeability | Medium |
| 17. Rotatable bonds | (No specific threshold; high flexibility may reduce binding) | Low |
| 18. Aromatic ring count | More rings → more planar surface → intercalation potential | Medium |
| 19. pKₐ (strongest acid/base) | (No specific cutoff; extreme pKₐ yields ionization, reducing uptake) | Low |
| 20. Redox cycling (catechol/quinone) | Quinone-like systems generate ROS and DNA damage | High |
| 21. Metabolic stability (persistent motifs) | Polyaryl/halogenated scaffolds resist metabolism | Medium |
| 22. CYP bioactivation potential | (No rule given; e.g. aromatic heterocycles prone to CYP) | Low |
| 23. DNA-binding propensity | Flat polyaromatics intercalate/hydrophobically bind DNA | Medium |
| 24. Genotoxic alert presence | Any of above functional groups implies genotoxic risk | High |

The confidence levels reflect the weight of evidence and consensus: **High** for classic, well-documented alerts; **Medium** for properties with less-direct correlation; **Low** for speculative/less-defined factors.
