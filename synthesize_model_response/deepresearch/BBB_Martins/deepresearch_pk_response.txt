# Predicting Blood–Brain Barrier Permeability: Concise Calculation Rules and Descriptor Cutoffs

## Executive summary

Blood–brain barrier permeability (BBBP) for **small molecules** is most consistently associated with the coupled balance of **lipophilicity vs. polarity** (logP/logD vs. PSA/TPSA), **molecular size/shape** (MW, volume, surface area), **hydrogen-bonding capacity** (HBD/HBA, heteroatom counts, H-bond acidity/basicity), and **ionization/charge state** (pKa-driven microspecies at pH 7.4).

In practice, BBBP prediction must also account for the **biochemical barrier**: efflux transporters—especially **P-glycoprotein (P-gp/ABCB1)** and **BCRP/ABCG2**—can negate otherwise favorable passive-diffusion properties. Binding (e.g., plasma protein binding and brain tissue binding) further decouples “permeability” from *effective* brain exposure.

The table below compiles **widely cited heuristics** (e.g., marketed CNS property guidelines, CNS MPO, BOILED-Egg) and **commonly used model descriptors** (e.g., E-state/chi/kappa/BCUT, surface charge fractions, 3D polar surface/electrostatic descriptors, topochemical indices) used across BBB QSAR/ML and physicochemical-rule approaches. Numeric values are shown only where stable, explicitly reported cutoffs exist; otherwise “threshold unspecified.”

## Evidence base and modeling endpoints

BBBP is reported using several endpoints that are related but not interchangeable. **logBB** is defined as the logarithmic brain-to-blood concentration ratio, while **logPS** (permeability–surface area product) is derived from in situ perfusion and is often treated as more mechanistically aligned to barrier permeation (less confounded by systemic distribution) than logBB.

Mechanistically, BBB selectivity reflects both a **physical barrier** (tight junctions restricting paracellular passage) and a **biochemical barrier** (metabolism and active transport/efflux). Passive diffusion is frequently treated as the major route for many small molecules, but active transport and efflux can dominate specific chemotypes.

## Notes on scope, applicability domain, and caveats

These rules are primarily suited to **small-molecule passive BBB permeation** screening; they are less reliable when **active uptake** dominates (carrier-mediated influx) or when permeability is **efflux-limited** by transporters. BBB modeling papers and reviews repeatedly emphasize that “simple” physicochemical rules capture only part of the in vivo complexity.

Endpoints matter: logBB can be confounded by systemic distribution and binding, whereas logPS aims to isolate permeability; both still conflate passive diffusion with unidentified transport components in many datasets.

Finally, many “descriptor” rules (topological indices, E-state, BCUT, shape/3D descriptors) are **model-dependent**: their interpretability and thresholds can shift with descriptor definitions, software implementations, curation of charge/tautomer states, and the experimental assay behind the dependent variable.
