# Executive Summary
Oral absorption (“HIA”) correlates with drug-like physicochemical properties. Classic filters (Lipinski, Veber, Ghose, Egan, Palm) define numeric cutoffs for molecular weight, lipophilicity, polarity and flexibility that favor high absorption. For example, Lipinski’s rule‐of‐5 (MW≤500; clogP≤5; HBD≤5; HBA≤10) and Veber’s criteria (rotatable bonds≤10; TPSA≤140 Å²) flag likely well-absorbed compounds. Extended analyses refined these: e.g. Egan’s filter (clogP≤5.88; TPSA≤131.6 Å²) and Palm’s dynamic PSA rule (3D PSA≤60 Å²) predict >90% human absorption. The table below compares all identified rules by source type and typical thresholds.

## Key Physicochemical Rules for High HIA

1. **Molecular weight ≤ 500 Da (Lipinski)**.
2. **cLogP ≤ 5 (Lipinski)**.
3. **H-bond donors ≤ 5 (Lipinski)**.
4. **H-bond acceptors ≤ 10 (Lipinski)**.
5. **Rotatable bonds ≤ 10 (Veber)**.
6. **Total polar surface area (TPSA) ≤ 140 Å² (Veber)**.
7. **cLogP ≤ 5.88 (Egan)**.
8. **TPSA ≤ 131.6 Å² (Egan)**.
9. **TPSA ≤ 63 Å² (Palm strict cutoff)**.
10. **Dynamic (3D) PSA ≤ 60 Å² (Palm)**.
11. **cLogP ≥ –0.4 (Ghose)**.
12. **cLogP ≤ 5.6 (Ghose)**.
13. **Molar refractivity 40–130 (Ghose)**.
14. **Atom count 20–70 (Ghose)**.
15. **HBD+HBA ≤ 12 (Veber alternative)**.
16. **Predominantly unionized at intestinal pH 6–8 (unspecified)**.
17. **High aqueous solubility (unspecified threshold)**.
18. **Lower aromatic ring count (unspecified)**.
19. **Intramolecular H-bonds to reduce polar surface (unspecified)**.
